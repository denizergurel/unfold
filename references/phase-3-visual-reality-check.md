# Phase 3 — Visual reality check: authenticity over generation

This phase resolves the visual placeholders from Phase 2. The governing rule:
**when you are showing product taste, authenticity beats generation every
time.**

## The hierarchy of visuals

Prefer, in this order:

1. **Real screen-grabs of the actual product** the person built. Full-bleed,
   high-detail, the real thing. This is the gold standard — it *is* the proof.
2. **Real footage / recordings** of the product in use, demos, launch moments.
3. **Real artifacts** — published articles, announcement pages, press, talk
   stills.

Avoid, unless there is genuinely no real alternative:

- **AI-generated imagery.** It reads as disingenuous when the whole point is to
  prove authentic taste.
- **Animated SVGs / decorative motion graphics.** They tend to feel off and add
  polish without adding proof.
- **Screenshots of text** (e.g. a wall of bullet points). They lack the "wow" of
  real product imagery and are hard to read at video size.

## Why this rule exists

The artifact's job is to let a viewer *see* what the person can build. A
generated image proves nothing about their work; a real screen-grab proves
everything. Substituting generation for proof quietly defeats the purpose.

## What to do

Audit each slide's proposed visual against the hierarchy. For any slide leaning
on generated or decorative imagery, flag it and ask whether a real artifact
exists. If proof-of-work visuals are genuinely missing for an important beat,
that's a signal to either source one or reconsider whether that beat earns a
slide.

## Output of this phase

Every slide has a real, assigned visual (or an explicit, justified exception).
No placeholder visuals remain.

## When the real artifact can't be shown (NDA / confidential)

Sometimes the realest proof is exactly the thing the user can't display. Don't
force it and don't fall back to generated filler. Options, roughly best to
worst:

1. **Public proxy.** A shipped, public-facing piece of the same work (a launch
   page, press, a public demo) stands in for the confidential internals.
2. **Redacted real view.** A genuine screen-grab with sensitive parts blurred or
   cropped — still real, just protected. Confirm the user is comfortable.
3. **Described, not shown.** A clean text/title slide that narrates the judgment
   while the narration carries the weight. Honest about what it is.

Never substitute a generated image *pretending* to be the real product — that
breaks the authenticity contract worse than an honest "described, not shown"
slide.

## Anti-patterns (don't do this)

- **Generated stand-in for real work.** Don't paper over a missing screenshot
  with an AI image of a fake UI. It reads as disingenuous and defeats the point.
- **Walls of text as "visuals."** A slide of bullet points is not proof of work;
  it's the resume you're trying to escape.
- **Pressuring past a confidentiality boundary.** If the user says it's NDA'd,
  use the options above — don't keep angling for the raw asset.
